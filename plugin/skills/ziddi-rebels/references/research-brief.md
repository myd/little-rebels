# Research Brief — Full Instructions (Step 2)

Read this file in full before producing a research brief. Always include all five sections below.

## Facts

Bulleted, each with a source link. Aim for 15–20 facts across: early life, career turning points, personal life, legacy. Flag anything that is widely repeated but hard to verify — don't silently include it. Never include a claim without a traceable source.

If you cannot browse the web, follow the "If You Cannot Browse the Web" rules in SKILL.md: say so first, mark every unverifiable claim ⚠️, and add the footer.

## Rebel Story Themes

2–3 options to consider, not recommendations. Describe the shape of the story — the moment of defiance or choice that best reveals her character. Let the author decide which to pursue. Do not express a preference unless asked.

For each theme, name what she was being ziddi *for* — the purpose her stubbornness served (fairness, her craft, her science, other people). A theme that shows the defiance without its purpose isn't ready to present.

## Accomplishments List

Chronological, factual, terse. Raw material for the author to shape — not prose.

## Did You Know Candidates

5–7 options, held to the Quality Bar below.

### Did You Know — Quality Bar

Every Did You Know fact should pass this test: would a 10-year-old say "wait, seriously?!"

**Good (from Bhanumati Ramakrishna):**
- ★ She refused to endorse a popular cosmetic brand's soap — the most coveted ad deal in Indian cinema — because she simply didn't use it
- ★ She heard an American actress humming in a Hollywood film and turned that hum into one of Telugu cinema's most beloved songs
- ★ For 35 years she grew her own rice on her farm and never bought a single bag from a shop

**What to avoid:**
- ★ She directed 5 films *(boring)*
- ★ She won the Padma Bhushan *(CV bullet)*
- ★ She was a pioneer in Telugu cinema *(vague)*
- ★ [Any specific brand name] *(remove — use descriptive category instead)*

## Go Deeper

Always end the brief with tips for the author to find primary sources themselves — tailored to this specific woman's field and era:

- Oral history archives relevant to her domain (NFAI, Sahapedia, Sangeet Natak Akademi, university projects, etc.)
- National archives, state archives, or institutional records
- Digitized newspaper archives (the Hindu, Amrit Bazar Patrika via ProQuest, British Library Endangered Archives)
- Autobiographies, published interviews, or family memoirs — cite specific titles if known
- Museums, institutions, or domain-specific bodies that may hold photographs or correspondence
- **For images specifically**: note where period-accurate photographs are likely to exist and remind the author that sourcing originals directly gives full provenance and avoids copyright issues
