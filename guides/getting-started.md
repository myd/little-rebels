# Getting Started — Your First Session

This guide walks you through what a first session with the Little Rebels assistant actually looks like, from "I have an idea" to "I have a research brief and a story direction." No setup knowledge needed — if you haven't installed the assistant yet, the [README](../README.md) shows you how, in whichever AI you use.

To make it easy to follow, we'll watch a fictional author — **Meera, a primary school teacher** — go through her first session. Your session will look very similar.

---

## Step 1: Name the woman you want to write about

Meera opens a new conversation and types:

> **Meera:** I'd like to write about Janaki Ammal for Little Rebels.

That's it. No special commands. The assistant recognizes this as the start of a new profile and — importantly — it does *not* dive into research yet. First, it needs to understand what Meera is making.

## Step 2: Answer the three kickoff questions

The assistant replies with three questions, all at once:

> **Assistant:** Lovely choice! Before I start researching, three quick questions:
>
> 1. **Who are you writing for?** What age band or reading level are you targeting?
> 2. **How do you want to work?** Do you want to write the story yourself (I'll stick to research support), or would you like me to draft sections for you to edit and react to?
> 3. **Do you already have a "rebel story" angle in mind** — a moment of defiance you want the story to center on — or should I surface options from the research?

Meera answers in plain language:

> **Meera:** Writing for ages 7–9. I'll write the story myself — just give me research. And no, I don't have an angle yet, show me options.

The assistant confirms it back in one line, so both sides know the plan:

> **Assistant:** Got it — writing for grade 3–4, you're drafting yourself, and you'd like me to surface story angle options. Let me pull the research brief.

**Why this matters:** everything that comes later — the reading level checks, the style feedback — is calibrated to the answers you give here. There's no "default" age group; you set it.

## Step 3: Receive the research brief

A few moments later, Meera gets a structured brief with five sections:

- **Facts** — 15–20 sourced facts spanning early life, career turning points, personal life, and legacy. Each one comes with a source link, and anything that's widely repeated but hard to verify is flagged rather than quietly included.
- **Rebel Story Themes** — 2–3 possible story shapes (more on this below).
- **Accomplishments List** — a terse, chronological list. Raw material, not prose.
- **Did You Know Candidates** — 5–7 surprising facts, held to a simple test: would a 10-year-old say *"wait, seriously?!"*
- **Go Deeper** — pointers to archives, memoirs, and museums where Meera can find primary sources and period photographs herself.

For Janaki Ammal — a botanist who worked on sugarcane in the 1930s — the story theme options might look something like this (abridged):

> **Option A — The scientist who sweetened a nation's sugar.** A story about proving that Indian sugarcane could be improved by Indian science, at a time when few believed an Indian woman belonged in a lab at all.
>
> **Option B — The woman who chose plants over expectations.** She turned away from the conventional path expected of her and crossed an ocean to study botany instead.
>
> **Option C — The guardian of the valley.** Late in life, she lent her voice to the movement that saved Kerala's Silent Valley rainforest.

Notice the assistant presents these as **options, not recommendations**. It won't tell you which story to write — that's your call as the author.

## Step 4: Choose your rebel story angle

Meera picks one:

> **Meera:** Option A. I love the idea of the sugarcane story.

> **Assistant:** Great — the story angle is locked: Indian sugarcane, Indian science. You're on Step 4 now: writing. Take your time with the draft. When you have one — even a rough one — share it here and I'll offer to run a fact-check.

And that's the handoff. Meera goes off and writes. The assistant waits.

---

## What happens after your first draft

When you paste in a draft, the workflow continues, one step at a time:

1. **Fact-check** — every claim listed and checked against a source. Nothing gets a pass without one.
2. **Reading level evaluation** — how the draft measures against your target, with the specific sentences pulling it up or down. The assistant flags them but never rewrites them — the fixing is yours to do.
3. **Style check** — flags for things like Wikipedia-ish phrasing or passive voice in the brave moments.
4. **Illustration prompt** — once the story is locked, a ready-to-paste prompt for an image generator, faithful to her real, documented appearance.
5. **Design spread prompt** — instructions for laying out the finished two-page spread.

At every step, the assistant tells you where you are and what comes next — so you're never lost in the process.

## A few tips for a smooth first session

- **One woman per conversation** works best. Start fresh for each new profile.
- **Don't skip the kickoff questions** — they take thirty seconds and shape everything after.
- **Rough drafts are welcome.** The fact-check and reading level passes are most useful *before* you polish.
- **You're allowed to change your mind.** Picked a story angle and it isn't working? Just say so — the other options are still on the table.

Ready? Open your AI, name your rebel, and start writing. ✍️
